Focus Taiwan News Channel, Central News Agency (CNA) report [edited]<http://focustaiwan.tw/news/asoc/201706080019.aspx>The Centers for Disease Control said [Thu 8 Jun 2017] doctors confirmed that a 45-year-old Ukrainian woman working in Tainan, southern Taiwan, has contracted Japanese encephalitis, the 2nd such case this year [2017].The patient, initially experienced a severe headache and fever, but lost consciousness and was hospitalized on [30 May 2017]. She was confirmed as having contracted Japanese encephalitis on [Wed 7 Jun 2017].The CDC said health officials have determined the patient has not received related vaccinations and not traveled overseas recently. They identified her daily routine as traveling from her residence to her place of work in Danei District with occasional shopping trips to Yujing District.The health authorities have tentatively ruled out the possibility that the woman contracted the disease at her residence. Currently, no one in regular contact with her has developed any symptoms.A previous Japanese encephalitis case this year [2017] was reported in Kaohsiung.The disease usually appears between May and October, with the 3 spotted mosquito [_Culex tritaeniorhynchus_ ?] the main carrier.The disease peaks between June and July and vaccination is most effective in preventing infection, the CDC said.From 2012-2016, 32, 16, 18, 30, and 23 annual cases of Japanese encephalitis were reported in Taiwan.[Byline: Chen Wei-ting, Lilian Wu]--Communicated by:ProMED-mail<promed@promedmail.org>[The Japanese encephalitis (JE) virus transmission season in Taiwan has begun. JE virus is endemic in Taiwan and has caused cases sporadically there during the May-October season. _Culex_ mosquitoes are abundant enough there to accomplish transmission of the virus from birds and swine to people.Japanese encephalitis is a vaccine-preventable disease; the affected individual above had not been vaccinated. The only measures to prevent infection are vaccination and avoidance of mosquito bites.Maps of Taiwan can be seen at <http://www.tmf.org.tw/index.php/maps-taiwan> and <http://healthmap.org/promed/p/193>. - Mod.TY]
